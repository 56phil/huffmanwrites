*A note before we begin: none of the people quoted below are real. None of this happened. It is a thought experiment, told in the style of a retrospective documentary, about a single counterfactual: what if the man who has spent forty years insisting he possesses the best brain, the best words, and the best instincts of anyone who has ever lived — actually did? What follows is speculative fiction. Treat it accordingly, and enjoy.*

---

Historians tend to agree on very little about the period that later became known, somewhat reluctantly, as the Genius Years. But they agree on this much: nobody was prepared for it. Not the opposition, which had built four decades of material on the assumption that it wouldn't need to update its material. Not the allies, who had learned to read chaos like weather and had no forecast model for competence. And certainly not the man himself, who — by every account gathered for this retrospective — was as startled as anyone to discover that his own hype was, this time, accurate.

"We were ready for anything," recalled a former deputy communications director, speaking on condition of anonymity because, again, she does not exist. "We had a rebuttal folder for chaos. We had a rebuttal folder for scandal. We did not have a rebuttal folder for him reading the entire appropriations bill on the flight to Mar-a-Lago and coming back with fourteen structural amendments, correctly numbered, that both parties adopted without changes. Nobody trains for that. There's no folder."

## The Handshake Heard Around the World

The first sign, according to most accounts, came early — a trade negotiation that was supposed to be theater. The pattern, everyone assumed, was fixed: big claims beforehand, a photogenic handshake, a deal that mostly restated the status quo with new branding. Instead, the delegation walked in and found a 400-page counterproposal, written overnight, that closed three loopholes their own trade lawyers hadn't found in a decade of trying.

"He'd read our position papers. All of them. Including the ones we don't show our own ambassador," said a foreign trade attaché who insisted she be identified only as "a woman who was in the room and is still professionally rattled." "He kept asking why nobody had just *fixed* the tariff schedule for soybeans years ago, since the fix was obvious once you looked at the actual customs data instead of the talking points. And it was obvious. That was the horrifying part. It had been obvious the whole time."

The deal closed in a single afternoon. Markets, which had priced in weeks of brinkmanship, did not know what to do with themselves. Cable news, which had booked six panelists to discuss the coming standoff, ran the segment anyway, out of institutional momentum, discussing a standoff that had already ended.

## The Debate Nobody Wanted to Talk About

Perhaps the most disorienting episode — cited by nearly everyone interviewed for this piece — was a debate performance so thorough, so procedurally devastating, that both campaigns quietly agreed afterward never to mention it again.

"He didn't perform. That's the thing people don't understand if they weren't there," said a former rival campaign's opposition researcher, who spent eleven months building a binder that went entirely unused. "Performing is fun. Performing gives you something to clip. He just answered the question. Every question. Completely. With the follow-up already anticipated and addressed before the moderator could ask it. It was the least entertaining ninety minutes of television in the history of the format, and it ended his opponent's campaign by Thursday."

Ratings, notably, cratered. A genuinely competent debate, it turned out, is terrible television. There is no gaffe to relitigate, no viral moment to loop, no outrage cycle to feed. The next morning's shows filled twenty-six minutes of combined airtime discussing the fact that they had nothing to discuss.

## The Staff Who Had Nothing Left to Manage

If there is a tragic figure in this account, historians tend to nominate the people hired specifically to manage disorder, who found themselves, quarter by quarter, out of a job.

"I was brought in to handle message discipline," said a former press aide, now — in this fictional universe — running a small bakery in Ohio, a career change she describes as "the direct result of there being nothing left to discipline." "You cannot manage chaos that isn't happening. I sat in meetings where he'd correctly predict, to the week, how a policy rollout would play in each individual media market, and then it would play exactly that way, and there was nothing for me to do. I brought a book to work. I finished forty books that year. I have never been so professionally humiliated in my life."

Several former rivals-turned-commentators described a similar sensation: the loss of a familiar opponent. An entire industry — cable panels, columnists, late-night monologue writers — had been built around a predictable rhythm of provocation and response. The genius years broke the rhythm without replacing it with anything easier to talk about. Competence, it turns out, does not generate a punchline. It generates a bill that passes and a room that quietly, uncomfortably, applauds.

## The Nobel Committee's Longest Meeting

By most reconstructed accounts, the Nobel committee's deliberations over a peace prize nomination ran four hours longer than any in the prize's history — not because anyone doubted the outcome had been earned, but because half the room had spent a career assuming this day would never come and hadn't prepared remarks.

"The brief was airtight," said a committee member speaking, like everyone else here, from a place that does not exist. "Two wars ended through actual, verifiable, boring diplomacy — the kind involving patience and reading — rather than declared victory. We were furious about how straightforward it was to approve. We wanted a harder decision. He did not give us one."

The acceptance speech, widely described as the shortest in the prize's modern history, reportedly ran ninety seconds and contained no references to ratings, crowd size, or historical superlatives. Several attendees said this was the single most unsettling moment of the entire period — not because it went badly, but because everyone had spent so long rehearsing how to react to the version of the speech they expected that nobody had a reaction ready for the one they got.

## What the Genius Years Actually Cost

Ask people what they miss least about the period before, and the answers are immediate. Ask what they miss most about the period after, and the room tends to go quiet.

"You'd think competence would be a relief," said the trade attaché. "And it was, materially. Fewer crises. Better outcomes. Genuinely. But there is a version of public life that runs on argument the way a fire runs on air, and when you take away the thing to argue about, it doesn't just get quieter. It gets *strange*. People didn't know what to do with a president who was right most of the time and boring about being right. We'd built our whole culture around the alternative."

Several historians, reflecting on the period, land on a similar note: the fantasy was never really about intelligence. The fantasy — the one embedded in the boast itself, repeated for decades, *best brain, best words, very stable genius* — was about a shortcut. A world in which being right could simply be declared, loudly, without the tedious business of doing the reading, sitting with the complexity, sweating the follow-through. The counterfactual imagined here isn't really a smarter man. It's the same man, with the work actually done — and the work, it turns out, was always the part being skipped.

Which may be the joke underneath the joke. We don't actually lack for genius, in the sense of raw capacity. We lack, fairly often, for people willing to trade the performance of certainty for the slower, less photogenic labor of getting it right. That trade has never required a higher IQ. It has only ever required patience, humility about what you don't know, and enough discipline to do the reading before the cameras roll.

The Genius Years never happened, of course. But the gap they're meant to needle — between what we say we value and what we're actually built to reward — is real enough to sit with for a minute after the laugh fades.
